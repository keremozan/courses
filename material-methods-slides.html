Material Methods

How to orient yourself to things in research, and why the question of what things are turns out to be surprisingly difficult

Kerem Ozan Bayraktar · February 2026

From "Material Methods: Researching and Thinking with Things" (Sage Research Methods, 2022)

01

Most social science research centers human experiences. We ask people about their meanings, opinions, memories. But the things that frame everyday life often disappear from view.

The Humility of Things

Daniel Miller (1987) uses this phrase to describe how objects shape actions and social relations while remaining unnoticed. A chair invites sitting. A door requires opening. We move through environments structured by things without consciously registering them.

If you want to research material culture, you first need to confront a question that seems simple but turns out to be philosophically complicated.

02

What is a thing?

You might think objects are self-evident. A table is a table. But Tim Ingold (2010) complicates this. His office is clearly filled with objects. When he goes outside and looks at a tree, is that an object?

03

When algae grows on a tree, is the algae part of the tree or something separate?

A jumper gets an oil stain that won't wash out. The stain becomes part of the jumper. The thing has been materially changed through its history of use and contact with other materials.

Where things begin and end turns out to be a question with no obvious answer.

04

The words you choose carry ontological weight.

Whether you write about "objects," "things," or "materials" implies different understandings of what the material world is.

Ontology

The study of what exists and how things exist. In material culture research, your ontological position determines whether you see things as fixed entities, as processes, as relations, or as something else entirely. This shapes what questions you can ask and what methods make sense.

05

Ingold (2010) prefers "thing" to "object" because of what each word implies.

Object Thing
Bounded, solid, separate from us A "gathering of the threads of life"
"Stands in our way" (Ingold) Invites participation and relation
Suggests completion, fixedness Suggests ongoing process, becoming

A house, on this view, is never a finished object. We live in it, repair it, change it continuously. It is a thing we are perpetually entangled with.

06

But things also have solidity. They resist us.

Ingold's view emphasizes becoming, flow, invitation. Yet if people experience something as a bounded "object" (solid, identifiable, separate), that experience matters for research.

Bjørnar Olsen (2010) argues that focusing only on relations means the properties and effects of things may be undermined. A table might be made of materials that were assembled and will eventually decay, but for a considerable period it is identifiably a table. Its existence as a table matters for how people interact with it.

Olsen (2010). In Defense of Things. Altamira Press.

07

Can you hold both positions? Fowler and Harris (2015), drawing on physicist Karen Barad (2007), suggest you can modulate between seeing something as an entity AND as emerging through relations.

Wave-Particle Duality

In physics, electrons behave as both waves (disturbances spread through a medium) and particles (localized points in space). You cannot observe both aspects simultaneously, but you can shift between configurations depending on your experimental setup. Barad uses this as a model for understanding things. Through your research methods and theoretical framing, things are configured in particular ways.

Fowler and Harris (2015). Enduring Relations. Journal of Archaeological Method and Theory.

08

Materiality

The properties and capacities that things have, and how these lead to particular effects. A wooden table can be sturdy, porous, warm to the touch, able to be carved. These properties shape what the table can do and how people relate to it.

But materiality cannot be reduced to fixed properties. Karen Barad (2003) talks about matter having an "ongoing historicity." Material properties emerge through relations with people, environments, and other things. Even if some properties seem absolute, which aspects of the material matter, and how they emerge through processes and relations, are what you need to understand.

See Drazin and Küchler (2015) for further discussion of emergent material properties.

09

James Gibson (1979) offers one way to think about material properties in context.

Affordances

What an environment or object offers, provides, or furnishes to an organism. A flat horizontal surface at knee height affords sitting for humans. A small hole in a wall affords entry for a mouse but not a cat. Affordances are relational. They exist between things and the beings that perceive and use them.

A table affords placing things on it, sitting chairs next to it. Material properties don't stand alone. They exist in relation to people, environments, and how people perceive them. This opens up the relations between things, people, and context in research.

Gibson (1979). The Ecological Approach to Visual Perception. Houghton Mifflin.

10

Things are not passive. We cannot just impose meanings onto them.

Alfred Gell (1998) distinguishes between primary agents (humans with intention) and secondary agents (objects with causal efficacy). A shoe designer cannot simply impose "sexiness" onto shoes. They work with fabrics and shapes, and the shoes come to carry the maker's intentionality.

But the shoes can also act back. If heels fall off or cause blisters, the materiality has thwarted both designer and wearer. The material resists the intention.

Gell (1998). Art and Agency. Oxford University Press.

11

Actor Network Theory

In ANT, agency emerges from networks of humans and non-humans.

Bruno Latour (1993) analyzed the Berlin key, a double-ended key used in Berlin apartment buildings. The key has teeth on both ends. To unlock your door, you insert the key, turn it, then push the key all the way through the lock. It falls out on the other side. You cannot retrieve the key without locking the door behind you.

The material arrangement forces you to lock up. You cannot separate "human agency" from "key agency." The behavior emerges from the network.

Latour (2005). Reassembling the Social. Oxford University Press.

12

Some theorists reject the word "agency" entirely and focus instead on what makes things vibrant.

"The capacity of things...not only to impede...the will...of humans but also to act as quasi agents or forces with trajectories, propensities, or tendencies of their own." Jane Bennett (2009), Vibrant Matter, p. vii

Bennett, a political theorist, argues that recognizing the vitality of matter changes how we think about ethics and politics. If things have force and trajectory, humans are less the masters of the world and more participants in it.

13

Do we even need the concept of "agency"?

Ingold (2007) suggests you only need agency if you've already severed things from the contexts that make them alive. If you understand things as being "in life" (part of entangled flows of materials), you don't need to look for life "in" things.

The debate is not settled. What matters for research is being explicit about your position. Are things agents? Do they have vitality? Or is agency the wrong question?

14

Material relationality is central to ANT, new materialism, and assemblage theory. But there's an important distinction.

Things IN Relations Things AS Relations
How do people relate to this object? What relations produced this thing?
How do objects relate to other objects? What are its material histories?
Things exist currently alongside other things How are relations between materials happening now?

A wardrobe could be approached both ways (see Woodward and Greasley, 2015). As a set of relations between items of clothing, and as things that are themselves constituted through relations.

15

Even if relations are infinite, you must draw boundaries around what you research.

Your theoretical framework helps define units of analysis.

  • Assemblage theory (Edensor, 2011) approaches a Manchester church as an assemblage containing smaller assemblages (a door, a pew) and being part of larger ones (the neighborhood, the city).
  • ANT (Yaneva, 2013) focuses on heterogeneous entities in architecture and how they connect, move, and transform.
  • New materialism (Barad) focuses on configurations that include your own position as researcher.
16

Theoretical Frameworks

Different theories lead to different research configurations.

New Materialism (Barad)

All matter is relational. Things are produced through "intra-action" (mutual constitution). Research apparatus is part of what is being studied.

Actor Network Theory (Latour)

The social is assembled through human/non-human relations. Follow the actors as they emerge, move, and connect.

Assemblage Theory (DeLanda)

Heterogeneous forms come into relations. Assemblages have emergent properties. Relations are tenuous and can be broken.

Objectification (Miller)

Dialectical relation between people and things. We externalize ourselves in objects, then re-appropriate them back into identity.

17

Theory doesn't have to come first.

There's an assumption that research starts with theory, which gives you ontology, then you develop methods. But this is only one route.

Woodward describes starting from something striking in the world, then using theories to develop research questions. For her wardrobe research, she noticed how fashion literature reduced clothing to "fashion," far removed from how people actually kept, wore, and passed on clothes. Theory came after, as a way to think about what she was looking at.

Henare et al. (2007) propose letting your understanding of what things are emerge from fieldwork itself, taking things as they are conceptualized and used in particular contexts.

18

Methods do not just access what you're trying to understand. They enact the world.

John Law and John Urry (2004) argue that methods help create particular realities. Methods are part of the configurations of what you research.

If you ask interview questions centering people's experiences and meanings, things become absent from the research. Material methods involve thinking about how the materiality of methods themselves can generate data. There is no fixed match between theories and methods.

Law and Urry (2004). Enacting the Social. Economy and Society.

19

Synthesis

When orienting yourself to things, four questions frame your approach.

  1. What do you think things are? Objects, things, entanglements of materials, entities in relations?
  2. What effects do you think they have? Agency, vitality, affordances, causal efficacy?
  3. Where do things end? Boundaries, relations to other things and environments?
  4. What is your unit of analysis? Assemblages, networks, configurations, objects, practices?

These questions are not answered once at the start. Methods themselves can provoke you to think differently. The relationship between theory, methods, and empirical work is iterative.
