Course Description

This course investigates drawing as both a technical practice and a process of critical inquiry. You will engage with drawing as a tool for analyzing and reshaping how we perceive objects, spaces, and ideas.

Combining observational studies, structured experimentation, and collaborative exercises, the course emphasizes drawing's capacity to construct, deconstruct, and transform visual forms. You will work with diverse materials, scales, and approaches to explore themes of fragmentation, reconstruction, and conceptual development. By the end of the semester, you will produce a body of work that reflects a deliberate and informed engagement with drawing as a dynamic and versatile discipline.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Demonstrate precision and nuance in representing objects, textures, and spatial relationships.
  2. Analyze and reinterpret visual forms through processes of deconstruction, abstraction, and transformation.
  3. Combine foundational drawing techniques with innovative approaches to create work that is both technically and conceptually rigorous.
  4. Create a cohesive body of work that reflects individual artistic concerns, informed by the semester's studies.
  5. Communicate the conceptual and technical underpinnings of their work through written and verbal reflections.

Course Structure

This course treats drawing as a way of thinking and researching. We move from basic observation skills to experimental materials, ending with a personal project. The class is divided into three tracks:

Creative Exercises

Critical thinking, awareness, conceptual development. Short, prompt-based activities designed to trigger ideas, access memory, or analyze personal inputs.

Technical Exercises

Motor control and mechanics. Repetitive drills and warm-ups meant to train the hand and eye.

Studio Workshops

Methodology and synthesis. Primary instructional sessions where the instructor demonstrates specific techniques.

Individual Project

A semester-long inquiry where you define your own creative questions. No fixed format; scope determined by the nature of your investigation.

Schedule

Module I Observation & Basics

Foundational skills in observation, line work, and spatial representation.

Week 1 Mapping and Line

Diagrammatic logic, line control drills, and cognitive mapping.

Week 2 Memory and Space

Linear perspective systems, grid construction, and memory-based object reconstruction.

Week 3 Surface and Topography

Tactile observation, value gradients, and texture rendering.

Week 4 The Body and Structure

Somatic scanning, gesture drawing, and skeletal analysis.

Module II Materiality

Expanding the drawing toolkit through wet media, color, and mixed techniques.

Week 5 Wet Media

Ink viscosity, automatic marking, and wet-on-wet techniques.

Week 6 Color and Saturation

Chromatic systems, pigment layering, and optical mixing.

Week 7 The Composite Image

Spatial interruption, material adhesion, and collage.

Week 8 Audio-Visual Translation

Sound notation, mark-making inventories, and material illusion.

Module III Context

Presentation, professional practice, and critical discourse.

Week 9 Scale and Installation

Large-scale armature construction and site-specific drafting.

Week 10 Seriality and Iteration

Image transfer techniques and evolutionary iteration of a single concept.

Week 11 Presentation

Archival standards, matting, and cutting techniques.

Week 12 Discourse

Peer critique and drafting the artist statement.

Week 13 Documentation

Lighting, photography of work, and exhibition layout.

Week 14 Final Exhibition

Final presentation and critique.

Studio Citizenship

The studio is a shared professional environment reflecting your contribution to maintenance, safety, and intellectual atmosphere.

Preparation Arrive on time with all necessary materials prepared. Failure to bring tools or paper prevents participation and disrupts the group workflow.
Maintenance Clean surfaces, properly store communal equipment, and manage waste. Leaving a mess for others is a failure of professional standards.
Contribution Active participation in critiques and discussions is mandatory. Offer honest, constructive feedback to peers and engage seriously with others' work.

Course Policies

Attendance You may miss up to two classes. For any missed class, submit a drawing reflecting the session's focus. Additional absences impact participation unless documented emergencies are communicated.
Punctuality Arrive on time. Late arrivals will be noted and may affect participation grade.
Preparation Come to class with all required materials ready. Being unprepared impacts performance and participation.
Accountability Take responsibility for your work and studio habits. Manage your materials, respect deadlines, and maintain focus during class.

Feedback & Support

Scheduled Formal feedback during critiques and one-on-one project discussions throughout the course.
Office Hours Calendar link provided for appointments. Schedule a meeting for detailed feedback on your work.
Informal Approach me or TAs during breaks or after class for quick questions or informal input.
Peer Take advantage of peer critiques and discussions for diverse perspectives.

Materials

  • Drawing Tools: Pencils (HB to 8B), charcoal sticks, compressed charcoal, erasers (kneaded and standard), pencil sharpener
  • Inks & Wet Media: Black and colored inks, brushes of varying sizes, small water container
  • Surfaces: Drawing papers in 35x50 cm and 50x70 cm (required every lesson); 70x100 cm sheets for certain sessions; textured paper, cardboard, or acetate as needed
  • Rulers & Grids: Ruler (30 cm or longer), acetate sheets for grid-making
  • Additional: Scissors, craft knives, adhesive (glue or tape), folder for transporting work
  • Optional: Colored pencils, pastels, markers for individual projects
