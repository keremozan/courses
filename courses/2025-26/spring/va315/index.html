Course Description

Images do more than depict; they shape systems of meaning, power, and perception. This course examines how visual media function within contemporary cultural, technological, and environmental contexts, exploring their roles in producing identities, mediating interactions between human and non-human actors, and representing global phenomena.

Drawing on foundational theories and building your own research methods, you will explore the production, circulation, and impact of images across digital and planetary contexts, developing critical skills for analyzing their influence on modern systems and relationships.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Analyze foundational theories and pivotal shifts in visual culture studies.
  2. Examine the role of visual media in constructing systems of meaning and power.
  3. Investigate the intersections of visuality, identity, technology, and cultural systems through contemporary examples.
  4. Synthesize theoretical and practical insights to design methodologies on contemporary visual phenomena, produce projects, and share them in creative formats.

Weekly Rhythm

Each week (except project module) follows a tripartite structure:

The Lens

Engagement with a foundational text that provides the conceptual vocabulary.

The Object

Examination of a specific, contemporary phenomenon.

The Tool

A practical workshop component where you will experiment with a specific technique.

Schedule

Module I Agency & Power

Establishing the image as an active subject with rights, desires, and power.

Week 1 What Do Pictures Want?

Inquiry into the "pictorial turn" and the vitalism of images. Moving beyond semiotics to iconology.

Readings
  • Berger, John. 1972. Ways of Seeing. London: Penguin Books. [Chapter 1]
  • Mitchell, W.J.T. 1996. "What Do Pictures 'Really' Want?" October 77: 71-82.
Week 2 Visuality and the Police

Visuality is defined as a tactic of colonial and military ordering. The relationship between the "overview" and authority.

Readings
  • Mirzoeff, Nicholas. 2011. The Right to Look: A Counterhistory of Visuality. Durham: Duke University Press.
  • Foucault, Michel. 1977. Discipline and Punish: The Birth of the Prison. Translated by Alan Sheridan. New York: Pantheon Books. [Part Three, Chapter 3: "Panopticism"]
Week 3 The Civil Contract

The political ontology of photography. The image as a space of relation. The crisis of the "citizen" in digital virality.

Readings
  • Azoulay, Ariella. 2008. The Civil Contract of Photography. New York: Zone Books. [Introduction]
  • Sontag, Susan. 2003. Regarding the Pain of Others. New York: Farrar, Straus and Giroux. [Selections]
Project Module Research Methods

Developing your analytical toolkit and methodology.

Week 4 Survey of Visual Methodologies

Research groups form. A comparative survey of analytical frameworks.

Core Readings
  • Rose, G. 2016. Visual Methodologies: An Introduction to Researching with Visual Materials. [Selections]
  • Woodward, S. 2019. Material Methods: Researching and Thinking with Things. [Selections]
Week 5 Crafting Methods

Non-representational methodologies.

Core Readings
  • Manning, E. 2015. "Against method."
  • Hübner, F. 2019. "Against Method? Common Ground?"
Module II Infrastructure & Materiality

The "hidden abode" of the image; resolution, file formats, energy, and labor.

Week 6 Circulation Economies

Resolution as a political quality. The "Poor Image" as the lumpenproletariat of visual culture.

Readings
  • Steyerl, Hito. 2009. "In Defense of the Poor Image." e-flux journal 10 (November).
  • Manovich, Lev. 2001. The Language of New Media. Cambridge, MA: MIT Press. [Selections on compression]
Week 7 The Algorithmic Image & Generative AI

The shift from indexical photography to statistical synthesis. Diffusion models and the "mean image."

Readings
  • Noble, Safiya Umoja. 2018. Algorithms of Oppression: How Search Engines Reinforce Racism. New York: NYU Press. [Chapter on image search]
  • Manovich, Lev. 2020. Cultural Analytics. Cambridge, MA: MIT Press. [Introduction]
Week 8 Digital Labor & Content Moderation

The human cost of the visual feed. Content moderation, clickwork, and e-waste.

Readings
  • Parikka, Jussi. 2015. A Geology of Media. Minneapolis: University of Minnesota Press. [Selections]
  • Roberts, Sarah T. 2019. Behind the Screen: Content Moderation in the Shadows of Social Media. New Haven: Yale University Press. [Selections]
Project Module Methodology Prototype
Week 9 The Methodology Prototype Deliverable

Research groups present their methodology.

Module III Attention & Machine Vision

Images that function autonomously or aggressively; capturing attention or operating machinery.

Week 10 Post-Cinema & The Feed

The rise of the "flow." Post-cinematic affect and the disintegration of narrative time (TikTok/Reels).

Readings
  • Shaviro, S. 2010. Post-Cinematic Affect. Zer0 Books. [Introduction]
  • Shaviro, Steven. 2016. "Post-continuity: an introduction." 51-64.
Week 11 The Attention Economy & Dopamine Culture

Sludge content, sensory overload, and the weaponization of the nervous system.

Readings
  • Crary, J. 1999. Suspensions of Perception: Attention, Spectacle, and Modern Culture. [Selections]
  • Citton, Y. 2017. The Ecology of Attention.
Week 12 Machine Vision & Operational Images

Images made by machines for machines. The separation of "vision" from the "human eye."

Readings
  • Paglen, Trevor. 2014. "Operational Images." e-flux journal 59 (November).
  • Farocki, Harun. 2004. "Phantom Images." Public 29: 12-22.
Week 13 Memetics & Viral Warfare

The meme as the "gene" of visual culture. Context collapse and information warfare.

Readings
  • Shifman, L. 2013. Memes in Digital Culture.
Project Module Student-Led Studios & Presentation
Weeks 13–15 Student-Led Studios

Research groups lead the sessions.

Week 16 Final Presentation

Final presentation of the Visual Research Processes.
